Era III, in one picture

The tracker dynasty

The single largest lineage in the knowledge base — fifteen tools in one connected graph, propagating JCH's "contiguous sequence stacking" idea from 1988 to 2019. Its keystone, Laxity NewPlayer, is the one member verified against a disassembly at ~99.9% accuracy. The line comes full circle: JCH himself is a credited collaborator on Laxity's 2019 SID Factory II.

What the connection scan found

Threads that cross the borders

The most telling findings are the ones where a tool jumped a group or a national boundary — surfaced by scanning which composers used which tools, then verified as a coherent scene cohort rather than coincidence. These are people connections, recorded as prose links, never as code lineage.

01

A Swedish editor, adopted by a British group

The four musicians of UK group Sonic Graffiti were the second-largest user cohort of System 6581 — the in-house tool of Sweden's Oneway.

02

A German tool, run by the Polish scene

The same eight Polish composers who used the native Hardtrack Composer were Reflex-Tracker's heaviest users too — a whole scene adopting a foreign tool.

03

The Ocean succession

Paul Hughes wrote his Ocean driver after Martin Galway left the company, "for the new guys" — and the same composers span both. A studio, not just an author, as a unit of history.

04

A tool's real owners aren't always its author's group

Olav Mørkrid built Digitalizer, but its heaviest users were the group SHAPE — three composers, led by Kjell Nordbø (357 files) and Kristian Røstøen (106) — not his own Panoramic Designs.
